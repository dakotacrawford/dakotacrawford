Cracking Down

Muncie Police Department fights to contain meth

by Dakota Crawford, Aric Chokey
| NEWS 233




B

lacked out windows, late night activity and exhaust fans are common indicators that a home is being used to produce methamphetamine. In Delaware County, these often run-down homes with exhaust fans in the windows are easy to come by.

Sniffing out and seizing these labs is a big part of Muncie Police Department narcotics officer Mike Nickems’ job. He and four other narcotics officers work to contain marijuana, bath salts and heroin in the area.

The team has already seized 55 meth labs in 2015 — that is more than all but two Indiana counties saw in total last year.

“We’re well on that pace to shatter what we did last year,” Nickems said.

Nickems said homeowners and concerned tenants in the area, thanks in part to extensive meth coverage by local media, know what to look for. Sometimes it’s a heavy traffic flow coming and going from a certain house. Sometimes it’s the stench that Nickems describes as a mixture of urine and Rain-X, a popular windshield washing fluid.

About half of the lab seizures at MPD are the result of a community tip, Nickems said.

“Some of it, we’ll get leads ourselves,” he continued. “Other times it’s the community reaching out because there are communities infested with meth and you know you got a lot of good people living in those neighborhoods and they’re sick of it.”

In 2011, just four labs were seized in Delaware County. In 2012, it was 62. Then in 2013 it was 109. The one-pot method makes it possible to cook meth by mixing a few common drugs, such as over-the-counter Sudafed, in a bottle. It’s easier now than ever.

No other county in the state has grown as much, or as quickly as Delaware County, though. It’s not clear to Nickems why the increase has been so much larger in the Muncie area than anywhere else in the state.

“I’m not going to deny there is a meth problem,” he said. but I think our numbers are higher because the community does reach out quite often. But there’s no hiding it that there is a meth problem here.”

A rising concern for Muncie officials is the costs associated with seizing a meth lab. It can take months to properly clean a residence and to make sure the property is livable. Nickems said a recent two-bedroom apartment cost the county more than $30,000 to decontaminate.

Bill Morgan, an inspector with the Muncie Building Commissioner, said most meth houses have to be stripped of all soft materials. Carpet and curtains, for example are stripped from the home during clean up. All furniture is thrown out.

Sometimes the furnace and air conditioning systems have to be trashed as well because the chemicals contaminate the duct work. This all adds up quickly for those who own the property, and though insurance will sometimes cover the costs, Morgan said it almost always takes an attorney to get that to happen.

“These property owners are facing many thousands of dollars to do the contamination,” Morgan said. “Frequently, some more thousands of dollars to get the place habitable again.”

If the homeowner is unable to make a home habitable again, the city might take over the residence and pay for the repairs. Sometimes it makes more sense for the homes to be demolished, Morgan said.

The biggest concern, Nickems said, is that citizens are taking a huge financial hit.

“It’s taxpayer dollars that the police department has to spend,” he said. “It’s also taking us away from other crimes that are happening.”

The one-pot method

While the number of reported meth lab seizures is rising in Delaware County, the entire state is also seeing a slight rise in one-pot labs.

In 2014, 90 percent of seized labs were one-pot method operations compared to 83 percent in 2013, according to data from the Indiana State Police.

With the one pot method, meth cooks are able to manufacture methamphetamine by combining pseudoephedrine, commonly found in cold medicine, with other household chemicals in a small and portable container, such as a 2-litre bottle.

“It’s small, mobile and it can be contained in just a backpack. You can walk down alleys and manufacture it now,” said Mike Nickems, a Muncie police officer in the narcotics unit.

All of the necessary ingredients can usually be found at stores such as Wal-Mart.

“That’s their one-stop shop,” Nickems said. “Now, they can use common chemicals which is like ammonia nitrate, ammonia sulfate, coleman fuel, some batteries, sudafed. They mix all of that together, which is that one-pot method, contained in a reaction vessel.”

The resulting product releases about three times more dopamine, a natural hormone that regulates pleasure, than cocaine.

The technique is an alternative to other methods, including red phosphorus synthesis, which produces a clear, odorless and deadly gas. It also required the use of anhydrous ammonia, an ingredient that is difficult to come by. Cooks often steal the substance from farms, adding another level of risk to the red phosphorus method.

The one pot method allows meth to be manufactured in a small area with ingredients that are easy to access.

Recently, Muncie police found one pot labs in a local Wal-Mart restroom and then another in the backpack of a man riding a bicycle.

“It’s a real eye-opener because it’s everywhere,” said Pat Hart, executive director of the Delaware County Coordinating Council. “When people think of a meth lab, they think of ‘Oh it’s a Dr. Jekyll/ Mr. Hyde and he’s got this big laboratory.’ It’s not like that anymore, they do it now in one bottle.”

Cooks who use the one pot method sometimes dispose of the used materials in public, which Nickems said still ends up falling under the umbrella of a meth lab.

“When people are done making meth, they will throw their remnants in a bag and leave it by the side of the road, or alleyways,” he said. “When those are discovered, it’s still considered a lab, even if no one is arrested for it.”

Hart’s job is to help educate the public on community issues, including drug abuse. She said she recently discovered a meth lab in her neighborhood a few blocks from her house.

After she noticed irregular traffic during late hours, she asked around about what the occupant did for a living. She was told he worked on computers.

“You know, people get busy and don’t pay attention to their neighbors anymore,” Hart said. “It’s reduced so small that it can be going on almost under your nose. People think ‘Oh that’s not in my neighborhood.’ Well I’ll tell you, it’s in all neighborhoods.”

The clean up

At least once per week, Bill Morgan has to walk into a seized meth lab and decide whether or not the building is livable or unsafe.

As a Muncie preservation officer, he inspects meth labs after Muncie police remove all manufacturing ingredients and products.

“What I’m looking for at that point is ‘Is this house habitable based on our housing code?’” he said.

Morgan said the houses end up with a lot of water damage from the amount of water used to clean the houses. He frequently finds houses that have been stripped of the wiring because the tenants sell it for scrap money.

Muncie police also usually remove all soft surfaces that have absorbed meth contamination including carpet, window insulation furniture and sometimes the furnace.

“If the contamination was fairly extensive and it was occurring in the winter months and the furnace was running, it has pumped the contamination throughout the heat system and so it just goes away,” he said.

If he finds the property to be unsafe based on the city’s building code, it is placed on the Unsafe Building registry and the city has to hire a licensed cleaning company to clean it more thoroughly.

That cleaning can end up costing homeowners thousands of dollars.

According to the Indiana State Police, clean up can average around $3,500 on top of the $17,000 in property damages.

Homeowners might be able to get help from their insurance coverage, but Mike Nickems, a Muncie police officer in the narcotics unit, said some companies are starting to include clauses to shield themselves from having to pay in the instance of a meth lab.

After a homeowner’s property is recognized as “unsafe” by Morgan, the city then sends a notice to them to talk about getting the building out of the unsafe status. The costs can be overwhelming for landlords who own property that is frequently used as meth labs.

Pat Hart, executive director for the Delaware County Coordinating Council said a couple from Canada recently drove all the way down to attended one of her organization’s forums on meth houses. The couple owns several properties in Muncie that had tenants who manufactured meth.

“They wanted to come in person to see what we are going to do to pay to get their houses cleaned up,” Hart said. “And the prosecutor was like ‘Well, we don’t do that.’ This lady got really emotional and wanted to know what we were going to do because it was going to be quite expensive.”
