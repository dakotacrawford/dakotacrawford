Facelift

How The Promenade is bringing a new culture to Ball State's Village

By Dakota Crawford

It’s just not the same in The Village anymore.

University Avenue is visibly different now that the Village Promenade stands four stories tall over its south sidewalk. Businesses and other homes in the area look a little older in comparison to the freshly-built luxury apartment complex.

Local business owners speculate that traditional mom-and-pop places in the Village could go under as new, fresh shops open up in the area. Dill Street Bar and Grill, once a mainstay for Village regulars, has seen its business dwindle since Brothers Bar and Grill opened in the first floor of the Promenade building.

There is a bright side for other businesses, though. Andy Goth, owner of Sunsations Tanning located across from the Promenade, received a $12,000 paycheck to make his tanning beds available to residents in the new apartments.

“[The Promenade] is going to spend some money on bulbs and electricity and they’re going to take up some space,” Goth said. “So in a way, they’re going to spend that money anyway. I’m not charging much more than what they would pay anyway.”

Goth said the Promenade was the first apartment complex in the area to use an actual tanning salon for its amenities. University Estates, an apartment complex on the North Side of Bethel Avenue, now uses Sunsations for its residents as well.

Goth said he hopes to shift his business model now that apartment companies are interested in paying for his beds. For him, this kind of agreement brings revenue and increases the volume of his customers.

On Jackson [Street,] and south of Jackson, for example, I think those houses are taking a hit. Those houses got a lot less attractive because of the Promenade.

Goth is also a 50 percent owner of The Cup, a four-year-old coffee shop on the west end of the Village. His business partner, Martin George, said the Promenade has its flaws.

“It’s about the same as it was a year ago,” Martin said. “We’re looking across the street at a row of empty storefronts, which is pretty much where we were at before the old building was torn down.”

The corner across from The Cup used to be home to Dill Street, a bar known for it's cheap beer and loud music. It was in a much smaller building that didn’t stand out from the rest of the Village quite like The Promenade does now. But George sees them nearly the same -- as buildings that are struggling to draw businesses into their space.

Other than a Frozen Yogurt shop set to open in January, there isn’t a business coming anytime soon. Despite some tangible benefits from the Promenade, Martin is skeptical about the long term sustainability of the massive building.

He’s lived in Muncie for 30 years and he's seen it change several times over. The hulking Promenade building, though, is changing how Muncie homes are being rented to college students unlike any before it. Goth said he owns several properties throughout the area and while some people won’t be swayed away from renting homes, Promenade is going to shift the market a bit.

“On Jackson [Street,] and south of Jackson, for example, I think those houses are taking a hit,” he said. “Those houses got a lot less attractive because of the Promenade. It was bound to happen. You’ve got the Village Promenade and the Grove now and people are coming closer to the Village.”

As more options in the way of luxury apartments become available in Muncie, local businesses and homeowners will be forced to adjust. Some, like Goth, are making out better than others. No matter how the market changes, though, Goth said one thing will remain true.

“It all boils down to what people want,” he said.
